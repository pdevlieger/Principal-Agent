Tit for Tat

An analysis tool

The Axelrod tournament is a pair of tournaments organized around 1980 by Rober Axelrod, professor of political science at the University of Michigan. The tournament was based around the Prisoner's Dilemma, a one-shot game, with payoffs as in the picture below. When both players cooperate, each player gets a payoff of 3. When both players defect, each player gets a payoff of 1. When players play different strategies, the person who defects gets a payoff of 5, the person who wants to cooperate gets a payoff of 0.

Axelrod solicited strategies from other game theorists to compete in a tournament by setting out a strategy. Each strategy was paired with each other strategy and a random strategy for 200 iterations of the Prisoner's Dilemma game, and scored on the total points accumulated through the tournament. The winner was a very simple strategy submitted by Anatol Rapoport called "Tit for Tat" (TFT) that cooperates on the first move, and subsequently echoes (reciprocates) what the other player did on the previous move. The results of the first tournament were analyzed and published, and a second tournament held to see if anyone could find a better strategy. Tit for Tat won again. Axelrod analyzed the results, and made some interesting discoveries about the nature of cooperation, which he describes in his book The evolution of cooperation.

It must be realized that there really is no "best" strategy for prisoner's dilemma. Each individual strategy will work best when matched against a "worse" strategy. In order to win, a player must figure out his opponent's strategy and then pick a strategy that is best suited for the situation. When played against more than one player, the optimal strategy depends on the population and the strategies they play. Furthermore, the best strategy depends on the payoffs and the amount of iterations the game is played. This website provides a way to tweak these parameters and play around with iterated prisoner's dilemmas. The following link compares the different payoffs of the tournament. Furthermore, it is possible to change the amount of iterations, the payoffs and the population that plays the strategy.
